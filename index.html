Why This Project Is Personal

My grandfather was diagnosed with Alzheimer’s disease. I remember him as someone who genuinely loved life, and then, slowly, I watched that change. He needed constant care and could no longer manage things he used to do without a second thought. That is not something I can reduce to a statistic, and it is part of why I care about getting this question right. Family history is one of several factors linked to higher risk (it does not guarantee a diagnosis), which means my generation, and the ones after us, have real reasons to take this seriously. I built this project, and the interactive model below, so that people like me and my family don’t have to guess: the same simulator used to evaluate a national policy can be revisited every year to help a family make an informed, current decision about early screening for itself.

Inside the Brain: What Changes with Alzheimer’s

Healthy brain tissue (light blue) gradually accumulates amyloid plaques and tau tangles (red), concentrated early in the memory and language regions — which is exactly why catching this process sooner, before symptoms appear, is the premise this entire model is built on.

Healthy brain tissue Amyloid plaque & tau tangle buildup

Why this matters for the model: plaque buildup is thought to begin years, sometimes decades, before memory symptoms are noticeable. The screening simulator in Section 3 exists to test whether catching that buildup early, before it spreads, is worth subsidizing at a national scale.

01

Overview and Research Questions

Alzheimer’s disease is one of the most expensive chronic conditions a health system can face: most patients are diagnosed only after significant cognitive decline, when care needs, and costs, are already high.

Early screening could catch the disease sooner, but screening an entire population is itself expensive, and not every early diagnosis changes a patient’s outcome or reduces later costs. This project is not a general survey of healthcare policy. It is a focused, data-driven investigation into a single question: at what screening cost, detection rate, and treatment-savings level does a national early-screening program pay for itself?

Sub-Questions

  1. How does the cost per person screened change the total cost of a national program?
  2. How does the detection rate of early-stage disease affect the healthcare savings the program generates?
  3. At what combination of screening cost, detection rate, and long-term savings does the program’s net economic impact turn positive?
02

Background: The Economics of Early Detection

🧬
Why Early Detection Matters Medically

Alzheimer’s disease develops in stages. Detecting it earlier allows patients to start treatment, plan care, and in some cases slow progression, which can delay the point at which a patient needs the most expensive forms of care (such as full-time nursing or hospitalization).

⚖️
Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (CEA)

Compares the cost of an intervention, here a national screening program, to the health and financial benefit it produces. A program is cost-effective when the savings and benefit it generates justify its cost, typically measured as cost per QALY gained or DALY avoided.

📈
QALYs and DALYs

A QALY measures years of life gained, adjusted for quality of life; a DALY measures healthy years lost to disease. Both can capture the benefit of early Alzheimer’s intervention.

🗂️
Data Sources

CDC, NIH/National Institute on Aging, World Health Organization, and World Bank Health Data. Every assumption in the model below is documented and adjustable.

What Other Governments Are Doing

This is not a general study of health systems — it’s a check on whether other countries have found subsidized screening worthwhile, and in what form.

CountryApproach
JapanRuns a national Medical Center for Dementia network (since 2008) for consultation, diagnosis, and early intervention; passed the Basic Act on Dementia (2024) defining government responsibilities. Some cities fund tablet-based cognitive screening tools directly.
NorwayFunds a national brain-disease research center focused on early-detection biomarkers; several municipalities run population-based dementia screening through general practice.
FranceRuns a National Alzheimer’s Plan with a National Alzheimer’s Data Bank tracking diagnoses nationwide.
United KingdomHas a national dementia plan and a research-matching helpline (Join Dementia Research), but does not mandate universal screening — citing the same inconclusive evidence base the U.S. USPSTF cites.
AustraliaFunds the National Dementia Action Plan (2024–2034) and the National Dementia Support Program, paying for information, education, and services rather than mandating universal screening.
United StatesNo dedicated national screening program. Cognitive assessment is part of Medicare’s Annual Wellness Visit, and the CDC’s BOLD Act funds state/local early-detection infrastructure rather than paying for tests directly.
Takeaway: most peer countries invest in diagnostic infrastructure, research, and support services rather than mandating universal population-wide screening — matching what this project’s model finds: cheap, broad screening is generally worthwhile, but expensive, universal testing is cost-effective only above certain detection-rate thresholds.
05

Eligibility Criteria

The modeled program targets the same population the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) and Medicare’s existing cognitive assessment already focus on:

  • Age 65 or older, community-dwelling, without a prior dementia diagnosis or recognized symptoms.
  • Enrolled in Medicare or an equivalent national health program.
  • Priority for the advanced biomarker follow-up test given to higher-risk individuals: family history of Alzheimer’s, cardiovascular risk factors (diabetes, hypertension, high cholesterol), depression, low educational attainment, or low social support.

Increasing age remains the single strongest known risk factor, which is why age 65+ is the baseline eligibility cutoff used throughout the model.

06

Precedent: How Other Diseases Got Mandated Screening

Several major cancers already have nationally recommended, insurer-covered screening that starts well before age 65. Alzheimer’s disease does not have an equivalent, even though it is one of the costliest chronic conditions in later life.

ConditionRecommended Starting AgeSource
Breast cancer (mammography)Age 40, every two yearsUSPSTF, updated April 2024
Prostate cancer (PSA)Age 40–45 for higher-risk men (family history, Black men, genetic mutations); age 45–50 for average-risk menAmerican Urological Association / SUO, 2026 guideline
Colorectal cancerAge 45USPSTF, 2021 (lowered from 50)
Alzheimer’s disease / cognitive impairmentNo mandated national program; USPSTF currently finds evidence “insufficient” to recommend for or against routine screeningUSPSTF Final Research Plan, June 2025
The project’s position: following the same public-health logic used to justify mandated breast, prostate, and colorectal screening, well before symptoms appear, this project argues that cognitive/Alzheimer’s screening should be added to the standard mandated preventive-screening schedule. Unlike those three cancers, Alzheimer’s screening does not yet have a USPSTF “B” grade recommendation behind it, so this is presented here as the project’s policy argument, not existing consensus. The simulator in Section 3 is the tool for identifying the cost and detection-rate thresholds at which such a mandate would be cost-effective, the same evidence a body like USPSTF would need before issuing one.
07

Estimated Program Cost and Responsible Department

Using the base-case assumptions (61.2 million eligible adults, 70% uptake, a $50 basic screening plus a $250 biomarker follow-up for 15% of those screened), the modeled program costs about $3.7 billion per year.

Cost scales heavily with the type of test used: cheap blood/cognitive-based screening stays in the low billions annually, while imaging-based testing (roughly $5,000+ per person) can push total cost past $200 billion per year — the range where the model shows the program stops being cost-effective unless detection rates are high.

10

A Personal, Year-by-Year Decision Guide

This project is meant to be reusable, not a one-time exercise. For a family like mine, where Alzheimer’s runs in the family, the same data and simulator above can be revisited every year to make a current, evidence-based decision rather than a one-time guess. Each year, a family could:

  1. Update the family risk profile: has anyone been newly diagnosed, has genetic or cardiovascular risk information changed?
  2. Check what has changed in screening technology and coverage: are biomarker tests cheaper or more accurate? Has insurance changed what it covers?
  3. Re-run the simulator above with updated cost and detection-rate numbers to see whether screening still looks worthwhile this year.
  4. Talk to a doctor about whether the family’s risk tier, especially a family history of Alzheimer’s, qualifies for earlier or more frequent screening (Section 5).
  5. Revisit the resources in Section 8 for updated guidance and support.

The goal is that my generation, and the ones after us, can use this same data-driven approach to revisit the decision annually — the way a family might revisit a financial or health plan each year.
